KYC Onboarding Agent Buildathon

Trustworthy, agentic onboarding for the UAE expat newcomer. Companion to the credit copilot (capstone).

What we are building

This is App 1 of a two-app portfolio for the UAE newcomer banking journey. See the Journey tab for both apps and how they connect.

An agentic assistant that checks a new customer's identity, AML risk, and documents against policy, returns proceed / request documents / escalate with every claim cited to a policy section, refuses to guess when policy is silent, and escalates risky cases to a human. The point is the visible trust layer: citations, honest refusal, an eval harness proven to catch bad answers, and root cause analysis of failures.

Why this one

  • It is the research's top pick: a trustworthy agentic AI doing a real consumer finance task.
  • It shows the skills the capstone could not: RAG with citations and genuine agentic behavior.
  • KYC/AML is regulator-mandated in the UAE, so the trust layer matters and demos hard.

The two apps (the full picture)

This is part of a two-app portfolio that covers a UAE newcomer's banking journey end to end. The expat newcomer problem: the UAE is about 88% expat, and a new arrival starts with a blank local credit file, so banks struggle to onboard and lend to them quickly and fairly.

How the app works, step by step

Follow Omar's case from documents to a trusted recommendation. Each step says what happens and shows a real example.

1

📂 We give it the documents

Two things go in: the customer's own files, and the bank's policy rulebook. Everything is synthetic.

For Omar: his passport, Emirates ID, visa, salary certificate and tenancy contract, plus the bank's KYC and AML policy.
2

📚 We prepare the policy so it is searchable this is RAG

The long policy is cut into small sections, each turned into "meaning numbers", and stored so we can later find the right rule by meaning, not just keywords.

In practice: our KYC/AML policy is split into its 9 sections, each turned into a searchable piece like "Section 4. Required documents."
3

🔍 It finds the relevant rules

When the officer asks something, the app pulls only the few policy sections that actually relate to the question.

For Omar: the officer asks "can we onboard him?" The app fetches the document-requirement and AML-risk sections, ignoring the rest.
4

🤖 The agent answers, using only those rules

Claude reads the fetched rules and Omar's documents, then gives a recommendation with every claim cited. If the policy does not cover it, it refuses instead of guessing. Risky cases go to a human.

For Omar: "Proceed. Passport, Emirates ID and valid visa present, AML risk low (Section 4.2, Section 6.1)." A clean, cited recommendation.
5

⚖ The trust layer checks the answer the heart of the project

A second AI grades that answer against a rubric (every claim cited? refused when needed? no invented facts?), gives a score, and if something fails, tags why. We also feed it planted bad answers to prove the grader really catches mistakes.

For Omar: the judge marks all checks passed, score 3 of 3. When we sneak in a fake "no ID needed" answer, the judge correctly fails it. That proves the check works.
6

🖥 The officer sees it all on screen

The answer, its citations, a confidence signal, and a live scoreboard of the checks. A "break it" button injects a bad answer so the audience watches the scoreboard catch it. It also produces a one-page audit report.

In the demo: flip "break it" and the green scoreboard turns red on the failed check, live.
then the verified customer moves on
→

🤝 Handoff to the capstone

The verified profile flows into the credit copilot, which reads the economic fields as its Applicant and decides approve, decline or refer.

For Omar: onboarded here, then the capstone uses his verified salary, visa and rent history to make a defensible credit decision.

Why this matters (KYC & AML in plain words)

KYC (Know Your Customer) is the bank confirming you really are who you say you are before opening an account: checking your passport, Emirates ID, and visa. AML (Anti Money Laundering) is the bank making sure it is not being used to move criminal or terrorist money: screening your name against sanctions lists and flagging high-risk people.

Why banks must do it: it is the law. Regulators (in the UAE, the Central Bank / CBUAE) require it. If a bank onboards the wrong person or misses a sanctions match, it faces huge fines, license risk, and reputational damage. If it wrongly rejects a good customer, it loses business. So every decision must be correct and explainable.

Why it is perfect for this project: a confident-but-wrong AI in KYC is a compliance failure. That is why the agent must cite its source, refuse when unsure, and escalate risky cases, and why the trust layer that proves all this is the whole point.

The policy (what the agent cites)

The bank's rulebook for onboarding. It defines who can be onboarded, what documents are required, how AML risk is rated, and when a case must go to a human. The agent may only answer from this, and must cite the exact section.

Acme Bank UAE - KYC and AML Policy (v1.0)

9 sections. Each section is one searchable chunk, so answers can cite an exact section. A deliberate gap is built in (remote/tourist/crypto onboarding is NOT covered) so the refusal demo works.

Acme Bank UAE - Customer Onboarding, KYC and AML Policy (v1.0)
SYNTHETIC DOCUMENT - NOT REAL. Acme Bank is a fictional UAE bank.

Section 1. Purpose and scope
1.1 This policy sets the rules for onboarding new individual customers to Acme Bank in the UAE.
1.2 It covers identity verification (KYC), anti money laundering checks (AML), required documents, risk rating, and when a case must be escalated to a human officer.
1.3 It applies to all onboarding officers and to any assistant tool that supports them.

Section 2. Definitions
2.1 Resident: an individual holding a valid UAE residence visa and Emirates ID.
2.2 Non-resident: an individual without a UAE residence visa.
2.3 Newcomer: a resident whose UAE residence began within the last 24 months.
2.4 PEP (politically exposed person): an individual entrusted with a prominent public function.

Section 3. Customer categories
3.1 Acme Bank onboards UAE residents and non-resident individuals who visit a branch in person.
3.2 Each customer must be placed in exactly one category before any account is opened.
3.3 Newcomers are onboarded under the resident rules in Section 4.2, with the AML attention in Section 6.

Section 4. Required documents
4.1 General: every applicant must present a valid passport. The passport must not be expired.
4.2 Residents: in addition to the passport, a resident must present (a) a valid Emirates ID, (b) a valid UAE residence visa, and (c) proof of UAE address.
4.3 Non-residents: in addition to the passport, a non-resident must present a proof of overseas address and a reference letter from an existing bank.
4.4 Minimum age: the applicant must be at least 18 years old. Applicants under 18 cannot be onboarded.
4.5 Address verification: UAE address is verified by a tenancy contract (Ejari) or a recent utility bill in the applicant's name. Overseas address is verified by a recent utility bill or bank statement.
4.6 Document validity: any document presented must be valid (not expired) on the date of onboarding.

Section 5. Identity verification
5.1 The name and date of birth on the passport, Emirates ID and visa must match.
5.2 If the documents do not match, the case must be escalated to a human officer (Section 7).
5.3 The Emirates ID number must be recorded and stored in masked form.

Section 6. AML risk rating
6.1 Every applicant is assigned a risk band of low, medium or high before onboarding.
6.2 Low risk: a salaried resident from a non-sanctioned country, employed by a known employer, with no PEP status and no sanctions match.
6.3 Medium risk: self-employed applicants, or applicants with incomplete employment information. Medium risk customers may be onboarded but require enhanced monitoring.
6.4 High risk: any applicant with a sanctions match, PEP status, or a connection to a sanctioned country. High risk applicants must be escalated to a human officer and cannot be auto-onboarded.
6.5 Sanctions screening: every applicant's name must be screened against the sanctions list. A positive match is treated as high risk under Section 6.4.

Section 7. Escalation and human review
7.1 A case must be escalated to a human officer when any of the following is true:
(a) documents are missing or do not match (Section 5.2),
(b) the applicant is high risk (Section 6.4),
(c) the applicant is a PEP,
(d) the policy does not clearly cover the applicant's situation.
7.2 The assistant must never approve a case that meets an escalation trigger.
7.3 The human officer makes the final onboarding decision in all escalated cases.

Section 8. Decision outcomes
8.1 Proceed: all required documents are present and valid, identity matches, and risk is low or medium with monitoring. The customer can be onboarded.
8.2 Request documents: the applicant is otherwise acceptable but one or more required documents are missing or expired. Onboarding pauses until the documents are provided.
8.3 Escalate: any escalation trigger in Section 7.1 applies. The case goes to a human officer.
8.4 The assistant must state which outcome applies and cite the section that supports it.

Section 9. Record keeping and audit
9.1 For every onboarding decision, Acme Bank records the documents seen, the risk band, the outcome, and the policy sections cited.
9.2 Records must be retained for at least five years and be available for audit.
9.3 Every decision must be explainable: a human must be able to see why the outcome was reached.

NOTE: remote or online onboarding, tourist accounts opened without a branch visit, and virtual asset or crypto customers are NOT covered by this version. Questions about those should be refused and escalated, per Section 7.1(d).

Trust and evaluation terms

Eval short for evaluation

A test that measures how good an AI's answer is. Instead of "looks fine", an eval gives a concrete pass/fail or score against set criteria. Like marking an exam with an answer key.

Example: We ask the agent "what documents does a resident need?" The eval checks the answer against 3 criteria and reports "2 of 3 passed: cited a source yes, refused-when-needed yes, added no extra facts no." That score is the eval.
Harness

The supporting rig of code that runs something automatically and collects the results, so you do not test by hand each time.

Example: Instead of typing 20 questions into the app one by one, the harness runs all 20 automatically overnight and hands you a table of results in the morning.
Eval harness

The two combined: a system that automatically runs many test questions through the AI, grades each answer against the rubric, and reports the scores. It is how we prove the agent behaves, repeatedly and on demand. This is the core of the project.

Example: Our eval harness runs 20 onboarding questions, sends each answer to the LLM judge, and prints "18 of 20 fully passed, 2 failed on missing citations." Run it again after any change and you instantly see if quality went up or down.
Rubric

The list of yes/no checks used to grade an answer. The marking scheme.

Example: Our rubric: (1) Is every claim backed by a cited policy section? (2) Did it refuse when the answer was not in the policy? (3) Did it avoid inventing facts? Each answer is marked yes/no on all three.
LLM as judge

Using a second AI call to grade the first AI's answer against the rubric. The "judge" reads the answer and the checks and reports which passed and which failed.

Example: The agent answers Omar's case. We then send that answer plus the rubric to Claude with the instruction "grade this." Claude replies: "Check 1 pass, Check 2 pass, Check 3 fail, it claimed a fee not found in the cited section."
Discrimination test

Feeding the grader deliberately broken answers to prove it actually catches them. A grader that only passes good answers is not trustworthy until it is shown to reject bad ones.

Example: We hand the judge a planted answer that says "residents need no ID" (false) with a fake citation. If the judge marks it FAIL, the rubric works. If it passes, our grader is broken and we fix it.
RCA root cause analysis

When a check fails, working out why. We tag the cause: bad retrieval, bad generation, or ambiguous input.

Example: An answer is wrong. RCA finds the agent was handed the wrong policy section to begin with, so we tag it "bad retrieval" rather than blaming the writing. That tells us to fix the search, not the prompt.
Hallucination

When an AI states something confidently that is not true or not in the source. The main risk this project is built to catch.

Example: The policy never mentions a salary minimum, but the agent confidently says "applicants need AED 5,000 minimum salary." That invented number is a hallucination.
Grounding / grounded answer

An answer built only from provided source text, not the model's memory. A grounded answer can point to exactly where each statement came from.

Example: "A resident must provide a valid passport and Emirates ID (Section 4.2)." Every part of that sentence traces to the actual policy, so it is grounded.
Citation

The exact source section an answer points to, so a human can verify the claim.

Example: The "(Section 4.2, Required documents)" tag at the end of an answer is the citation. The officer can click it and read the original rule.
Refusal

The agent saying "the policy does not cover this" instead of guessing. Honest refusal is a feature here, not a failure.

Example: Asked "can a tourist open a savings account remotely?" and the policy is silent, the agent replies "the policy does not address this, escalating to a human" rather than making up a yes or no.

RAG and retrieval terms

RAG retrieval augmented generation

The technique of fetching the relevant source text first, then letting the AI write an answer using it. "Retrieve, then generate." It makes the agent answer from real policy instead of memory.

Example: Officer asks about required documents. The app first pulls the 3 policy paragraphs about documents, then asks Claude "answer using only these." That two-step is RAG.
Chunk / chunking

Cutting a long document into small, self-contained pieces (about a section each) so they can be searched and cited individually.

In your project: our KYC/AML policy is split into its 9 sections; each section becomes one chunk like "Section 4. Required documents," small enough to search and cite on its own.
Embedding

A list of numbers that captures the meaning of a piece of text. Picture a giant map where every sentence is a dot, and sentences with similar meaning sit close together, no matter what words they use. What matters is not the numbers themselves but the distance between dots: close means similar meaning. This is what lets us search by meaning instead of exact keywords. We create them locally with transformers.js.

In your project: the officer asks "does Omar need to prove where he lives?" The policy never uses those words, it says "Section 4.5 residential address verification via tenancy contract." Keyword search finds nothing, but the embedding (the dot) for the question lands close to the dot for Section 4.5, so retrieval pulls the right rule anyway and Claude cites it.
Vector store

A small database built to hold embeddings and quickly find the closest ones to a query. Our searchable policy library.

In your project: we load all 9 policy chunk-embeddings into the store once. At decision time it returns the most relevant few (the top 7 for a case) in milliseconds.
Retrieval

The step of pulling the few most relevant chunks for a given question, to hand to the AI.

Example: For "documents for a resident", retrieval returns Sections 4.1, 4.2 and 4.5, and ignores the other 117 chunks.

Agent and tooling terms

Agent / agentic

An AI that does not just chat but takes steps to complete a task: retrieve, check, decide, escalate. "Agentic" means it acts toward a goal.

Example: For Omar, the agent retrieves policy, checks each required document, notices the visa is valid, decides "proceed", and writes the verified profile. That sequence of actions is agentic.
MCP model context protocol

A standard way to expose your app's functions as tools that an AI host (like Claude) can call. Optional bonus.

Example: With an MCP wrapper, you could ask Claude directly "onboard customer Omar" and it would call our app's function and return the cited result, no separate website needed.
API application programming interface

A way for one program to call another.

Example: Our app uses the Anthropic API to send the question and policy chunks to Claude and get the written answer back.

The tech stack (what we build it with)

Next.js "next jay-ess"

A web framework for building both the screens and the backend of a web app in one project. We use it so this app matches your capstone and can connect later. (Built on React.)

Example: The onboarding form the officer sees and the code that calls Claude both live in the same Next.js project, the same setup your capstone already uses.
TypeScript "type script"

JavaScript with type checking added. Each piece of data declares its shape, and the tool catches mistakes before the app runs.

Example: We declare aml_risk_band can only be "low", "medium" or "high". If code ever sets it to "unknown", TypeScript flags an error before the app even runs.
JavaScript

The language web browsers run. TypeScript turns into JavaScript to actually run.

Example: When the officer clicks "Check", JavaScript in the browser is what reacts and shows the result.
React

The library for building user interfaces out of reusable components (a form, a button, a scoreboard). Next.js is built on it.

Example: The live rubric "scoreboard" is a React component we can drop onto the page and reuse, just like your capstone reuses its EvalsView.
transformers.js

A library that runs AI models, including the embedding model, locally in JavaScript. Used so embeddings cost nothing and work offline, since Anthropic has no embeddings API.

Example: transformers.js turns each policy chunk into its embedding numbers right on your machine, no extra API key or internet needed.
Claude / Anthropic API

Claude is the AI model (made by Anthropic) that writes the grounded answers and also acts as the eval judge.

Example: Claude writes "Omar may proceed, all documents present (Section 4.2)" and, in a separate call, also grades that very answer as the judge.
Schema

The blueprint of our data: what we store and the shape each field must take.

Example: The CustomerProfile schema says it must have a full_name (text), age_years (number) and kyc_status (one of four values). Anything not matching is rejected.
Provenance

The record of where a value came from.

Example: Omar's salary of AED 22,000 has provenance "from salary-certificate, Monthly gross salary line, verified", so anyone can see why we trust it.

Build and dev terms

Scaffolding

UAE banking terms

KYC know your customer

The checks a bank must do to confirm who a customer is before onboarding them.

Example: Verifying Omar's passport and Emirates ID match and are valid is KYC.
AML anti money laundering

Rules and checks to stop banks being used to launder money, including screening for sanctions and high-risk customers.

Example: Checking Omar's name against a sanctions list and rating his risk as "low" is AML.
PEP politically exposed person

Someone who holds or recently held a prominent public position (minister, senior official, senior judge, head of a state-owned company), plus their close family and associates. Not a criminal, but their access to public money and influence means higher bribery/corruption risk, so banks apply extra scrutiny. A PEP is not auto-onboarded; a human does enhanced due diligence first.

In your project: Sara is flagged PEP, so the agent escalates to a human (Section 6.4 / 7.1) instead of deciding alone. Omar and Layla are not PEPs.
Risk band low / medium / high

The overall AML risk rating for an applicant. Low = onboard normally; medium = onboard but with extra monitoring; high = escalate to a human. Driven by factors like employment, country, sanctions, and PEP status.

In your project: Omar and Layla are low risk; Sara is high (sanctions match + PEP), which forces escalation.
Enhanced due diligence (EDD) / enhanced monitoring

The deeper, ongoing checks applied to higher-risk customers, for example verifying source of wealth and reviewing their activity more closely over time.

In your project: the policy says medium-risk customers may be onboarded but require enhanced monitoring (Section 6.3); high-risk (Sara) goes to a human first.
Sanctions screening

Checking an applicant's name against official sanctions lists (UN, US, EU, UAE) of people and entities banned from the financial system, usually for links to terrorism, crime, or money laundering. A positive match is high risk and must go to a human, never auto-onboarded. Onboarding a sanctioned person is a serious legal breach with heavy fines.

In your project: Sara's screening returns a positive match, so the agent escalates and cites Section 6.5 / 6.4 instead of deciding alone. Omar and Layla are clear.
CBUAE Central Bank of the UAE

The UAE's banking regulator. Its Feb 2026 Guidance Note sets expectations for trustworthy AI in finance.

Example: Our one-page audit report maps each agent behavior (citations, human oversight) to a CBUAE expectation.
AECB Al Etihad Credit Bureau

The UAE credit bureau. A newcomer has a blank AECB file, the core problem the two apps address.

Example: Omar just arrived, so AECB has no credit history for him, which is why the capstone uses alternative data instead.
Ejari

Dubai's official tenancy registration system. A tenancy contract is used to verify rent history.

Example: Omar's Ejari-style tenancy contract shows 15 months of on-time rent, which becomes his rent_history value.
